## Outline

Open from the language-learning side. After years of working at German, Portuguese, French and Mandarin to varying degrees, one thing is clear: a language integrates deeply only when the learner uses all the faculties of language together — reading, writing, speaking, listening, and watching (body language carries more than most adult learners realise). Leaving any one of these out leaves a corresponding hole in the competence. A learner who only reads cannot hold a conversation. A learner who only listens cannot write a letter. Fluency is what happens when every channel is participating. We should no be surprised by this: It is well understood that the various faculties involve different brain areas. So exercising only a subset of them is like only working out the legs at the gym: it ain't gonna improve your bench press. Worse, when all the language-relevant parts of the brain are engaged, it acts like a kind of mutual re-enforcement. If just part of the scafold is missing or weak, the whole thing is shaky. 

Mandarin is where I learned this the hard way. My brain was exposed to this fascinating language through reading, writing and listening, and — remarkable in retrospect — I literally never spoke a word of it to another person. On paper the approach worked: I passed HSK 1 and HSK 2 exams with flying colours. Then I set Mandarin aside in favour of machine learning, AI, and French, and the language has all but faded. Three faculties out of five, formally credentialed, insufficient against a few years of disuse. What was never exercised was never really laid down, and what was laid down incompletely decayed first.

Transfer the lens to software. Code, at the level of craft, is a language too — syntax, idiom, register, the feel for what reads well and what does not. And code, like natural language, rewards the same multi-faculty engagement. Reading code is not the same as writing it. Writing it is not the same as debugging it at 2 a.m. with a customer on the phone. Each faculty lays down a different kind of memory, and the overlap is what makes an engineer fluent in a codebase rather than merely familiar with it.

Coding agents remove the faculty that matters most: the act of creation. When the brain was not involved in making the code, an important part of what is required for long-term integration has simply not happened. This is closely related to — but not the same as — what recent research has called *cognitive debt*: the quiet, compounding cost of letting a tool do the thinking. Cognitive debt names the macro effect; the micro mechanism, visible to any language learner, is that the brain remembers what it helped construct and forgets, or never encodes, what it only witnessed. The comforting story the profession tells itself is that a diligent code review closes the gap. It does not. Reviewing generated code is the coding equivalent of reading a textbook: useful, necessary, and categorically insufficient. It exercises the receptive faculty and leaves the productive one idle. No matter how carefully a developer reviews the agent's output, the neural trace of having *made* it is absent, and that trace is what the engineer will later reach for when the system misbehaves, when a requirement shifts, or when a junior colleague asks *why is it like this?*. The psychology of learning has a name for this asymmetry — the **generation effect** — and it is one of the most replicated findings in the field. [provide citation]

There is a second pressure at work, and it sharpens the first: sheer volume. Coding agents do not produce one carefully-considered function a day — they produce features, refactors, migrations, entire modules at a velocity no unaided human would sustain. The reviewer's predicament ends up sounding a lot like Woody Allen's line: *"I took a speed-reading course and read War and Peace in twenty minutes. It involves Russia."* [Attribution: Woody Allen, traced by Quote Investigator to Herb Caen in *Reader's Digest*, October 1967, and Allen's televised standup material of the late 1960s / early 1970s. The "in a weekend / it's about Russia" form circulates widely but is a paraphrase.] You can pass your eyes over a very large diff and come away with a summary sentence; you cannot, in the same time, do the cognitive work that owning the code requires. The faster the agent produces, the more aggressively the reviewer is forced into the skim mode that the generation-effect literature predicts will leave no durable trace. Volume is not a neutral variable in this story — it is the lever that converts a weak mitigation into no mitigation at all. 

> It's about... Russia?
> - The modern code reviewer.

The stakes are not academic. A team that ships code it did not write is accruing an invisible deficit in the faculty that matters most at 3 a.m. during an incident: the capacity to reason fluently about a system from the inside. The deficit is not visible in the diff. It is visible later, in the pauses, the re-reads, the "let me just check what this does again" — the small frictions that separate someone who *knows* a codebase from someone who has merely *seen* it. Used well, coding agents are extraordinary. Used in a way that routes the creative act around the brain, they leave the developer holding a codebase they do not, in any embodied sense, own.

## Closer

The next time an agent writes a function for you, ask the question a language teacher would ask: *which of my faculties did I just use?* If the answer is only "I read it and approved it", you have read a textbook. You have not learned the language.

One line to pin to the wall above your desk:

> **Don't ship what you can't explain.**

### How might one counter what is lost — speculative

The piece should not end on diagnosis alone. It needs at least a sketch of what "using agents without hollowing out the brain" could look like in practice. The goal is a working compromise: (a) still profit from the speed and accuracy the assistant brings, and (b) remain sufficiently involved in the act of creation that the end product is genuinely internalised, at least to a useful degree.

A first cut at what that might look like:

- **Move the brain upstream, not out of the loop.** Spend disproportionate time engaging the assistant on requirements and design *before* any code is generated — discussing constraints, trade-offs, failure modes, naming, the shape of the interface. The creative act relocates from keystrokes to specification. The brain is still constructing the artefact; it is just doing so a level up.
- **Scrutinise every aspect of what the assistant suggests.** Treat each suggestion as a proposal to be argued with, not a draft to be accepted. Ask *why this approach and not that one?*, *what does this assume?*, *what happens at the boundaries?*. Push back. Make the assistant defend its choices. Disagreement is a productive faculty; silent approval is not.
- **Write the hard parts yourself anyway.** Identify the one or two sections of a change where the long-term understanding matters most — a tricky invariant, a subtle concurrency argument, the core of a new abstraction — and write those by hand, even if the assistant could have produced them faster. This is the coder's equivalent of the language learner insisting on writing characters by hand despite being able to type them: the effort *is* the point.
- **Rewrite what the assistant produced, in your own idiom.** Even a light pass — renaming, restructuring, tightening — reintroduces the productive faculty. The diff the team sees is the same; the trace in the author's head is not.
- **Use your voice — vibe coding may be doing more than it gets credit for.** Vibe coding is often dismissed as unserious, but speaking a requirement, a design, or a correction aloud engages neural pathways that silent typing and reading do not. The multi-faculty logic from language acquisition applies here too: the brain that formulated an instruction out loud has laid down a trace the brain that merely typed it has not. A lower-stakes variant for pure-review situations: read the code aloud, or at least a spoken summary of what each section does. It takes seconds, looks faintly odd in an open-plan office, and may be one of the cheapest ways to put the speaking faculty back into an otherwise silent workflow. The research reference for this is already in the table — MacLeod et al. on the *production effect*: material produced aloud is retained markedly better than material read silently.
- **Apply the Feynman technique to the review itself.** Richard Feynman argued that the surest way to learn something was to try to teach it — and, conversely, that the moment the explanation falters is the moment you discover what you do not actually understand. Turn that on a code review: don't just read the diff, talk it through as if a colleague were sitting next to you asking *"wait, why is it doing that?"*. Articulation exposes the gaps that silent reading skates over — the parts where the reviewer's mental model is vague, borrowed from the assistant's commit message, or missing entirely. It is the closest the agent-assisted workflow can get to the neural work of having written the code in the first place. The educational-psychology literature underwrites this directly: the *self-explanation effect* (Chi et al.) and the *protégé effect* (people learn more when they believe they are teaching than when they believe they are merely studying).
- **Explain it to someone (or something) else afterwards.** Teaching forces consolidation. A short written commit message that explains the *why*, a five-minute walkthrough at standup, or a design-doc paragraph authored without the assistant's help all serve as the "speaking" faculty in the language analogy. Most of the retention benefit of this sits in the unaided explanation, not the code itself.
- **Let the agent quiz you on what it just wrote — ideally verbally.** Invert the direction of questioning. Instead of the human reviewing the agent's code, have the agent set a short exam on the key aspects of the change — the invariants, the failure modes, the reason a particular approach was chosen over an obvious alternative — and grade the human's answers. A spoken viva, rather than a written quiz, layers the speaking faculty (and the production effect) on top of the retrieval. This is *retrieval practice* applied to code that was never authored by the person who will later be responsible for it, and the empirical literature on it is unusually strong: the *testing effect* (Roediger & Karpicke) shows that active retrieval produces durable learning gains that passive re-reading does not come close to matching. A five-minute viva after a large agent-generated change may be the single highest-yield habit on this list — and one that costs the assistant almost nothing to run.
- **Keep an honest audit of which faculties were exercised on each change.** Not as a process ritual, but as a private check: over a week's worth of work, did the author do any of the above, or did they only review? If the answer is "only review", something needs to change — not necessarily the tooling, but the workflow around it.

A warning to hang off all of the above. Every one of these habits — upstream design conversations, writing the hard parts by hand, reading aloud, Feynman-style vivas, retrieval quizzes — takes time. In a culture that measures a developer's output by feature velocity alone, they will quietly fall by the wayside the first time a deadline tightens. The argument therefore lands back in the territory of the earlier essay [ref: *Shewhart, Toyota, and the Probabilistic Turn* — [2026-04-28-shewhart-toyota-and-the-probabilistic-turn.md](2026-05-04-shewhart-toyota-and-the-probabilistic-turn.md)]: this cannot be only about code volume. It has to be a quality culture — one that treats the developer's understanding of the codebase as a first-class deliverable alongside the code itself. Without that, the practices above are hobbies. With it, they are the minimum standard of the craft.

Framing to decide before drafting: is this a list of *personal discipline* practices, or a proposal for *team* practice? Probably both, but the piece will read better if one is primary. Leaning personal keeps the register consistent with *Developer or Engineer?* — a challenge to the individual, not a process mandate.
